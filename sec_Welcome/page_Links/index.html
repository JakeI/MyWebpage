What I find Interesting (online)

The amount of information available online can sometimes make it hard to separate the sheep from the goats and find truly excellent sources. So here is a list of some online resources I consider both helpful and surprisingly hard to find:

Deep Learning

  • Siraj Raval's YouTube Channel is an excellent overview of deep-learning, neural-networks and TensorFlow .
  • Christopher Olah's blog is all about amazing visualizations for all kinds of neural-networks and deep learning methods.
  • Distill is a Blog regularly updated by a hand full of people and features many computationally intensive inline visualizations for deep learning problems.

3D Graphics in modern OpenGL

  • ThinMatrix is an amazing YouTube channel, featuring a regular video blog about the development of the videogame Equlinox, excellent high quality tutorials on general OpenGL, and Light Weight Java Game Library related tutorials.
  • Opengl-Tutorial.org is just what it's domain name sounds like.
  • Ogldef contains a great sequence of tutorials.

Didactics

  • ( ⚠ predominantly in German) Jörn Loviscach is a professor of Computer Scinece and Mathematics. He successfully implemented a teaching method called "flipped classroom" (or "inverted classroom"). His page contains a searchable list of more than 3000 Khan-style educational videos dealing with almost any mathematics or computer science topics commonly covered in a first year at university. His experience (frequently shared in public talks) is a constant source of inspirations to me.
  • ( ⚠ predominantly in German) Christian Spannagel is a professor of Commuter Science and practices an unorthodox teaching style similar to "flipped classroom". He regularly blogs about any work related topics including his teaching.

Simulation

  • My Physics Lab is a collection of simulations for basic physics problems.
